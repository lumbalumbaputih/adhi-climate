--- Three-sentence CV blurb (drop-in) ---
I analysed 75 years of daily rainfall from seven long-record South West Western
Australian stations (GHCN-Daily / Bureau of Meteorology observations) in Python,
along with the Indian Ocean Dipole, Southern Annular Mode and ENSO indices, to
measure the region's cool-season rainfall decline as an AASB S2 chronic
physical-risk assessment. I found a real, not-just-chance long-term decline of
about 2.9% per decade in April to October rainfall (Mann-Kendall p=0.001; a
standard test for whether a trend is genuine), a step down around the year 2000
(a sudden, lasting drop, confirmed by the Pettitt change-point test, p=0.006) that
left 2000 to 2024 about 17% drier than the 1950 to 1974 baseline (with the
regional mm series adjusted for changing station availability), and a faster
decline of roughly 4.4% per decade in the May to July early-winter peak, all
consistent across the seven stations and with published CSIRO and Bureau of
Meteorology figures. I delivered a fully reproducible analysis with every
statistical routine (Mann-Kendall, Sen's slope, OLS regression, Pearson correlation
and the Pettitt change-point test) coded from scratch and unit-tested, plus five
publication-quality charts, published on GitHub.


--- Interview headline (say this in about 15 seconds) ---
"I measured how much South West WA's winter rainfall has fallen. Over 1950 to
2024 the April-to-October total fell about 3% a decade, with a clear step down
around 2000 that leaves the last 25 years roughly 17% drier than the
mid-century baseline. Every one of the seven stations shows it. The decline
itself isn't controversial; what carries nuance is the cause, which the science
puts down largely to a strengthening high-pressure ridge and shifting storm
tracks driven by greenhouse gases and ozone loss. For a water utility or a farm
lender that's a textbook case of AASB S2 chronic physical risk: a permanent shift
in the baseline, not a run of bad years."
