--- Three-sentence CV blurb (drop-in) ---
I analysed 75 years of daily rainfall from seven long-record South West Western
Australian stations (GHCN-Daily / Bureau of Meteorology observations) in Python,
alongside the Indian Ocean Dipole, Southern Annular Mode and ENSO indices, to
quantify the region's cool-season rainfall decline as an AASB S2 chronic
physical-risk assessment. I found a statistically significant long-term decline
of about 2.9% per decade in April to October rainfall (Mann-Kendall p=0.001),
a step down around the year 2000 (Pettitt p=0.006) that left 2000 to 2024 about
19% drier than the 1950 to 1974 baseline, and a faster decline of roughly 4.4%
per decade in the May to July early-winter peak, consistent across all seven
stations and with published CSIRO and Bureau of Meteorology figures. I delivered
a fully reproducible analysis with all statistical routines (Mann-Kendall,
Sen's slope, OLS regression, Pearson correlation and the Pettitt change-point
test) implemented from first principles and unit-tested, plus five
publication-quality charts, published on GitHub.


--- Interview headline (say this in about 15 seconds) ---
"I measured how much South West WA's winter rainfall has fallen. Over 1950 to
2024 the April-to-October total declined about 3% a decade, with a clear step
down around 2000 that leaves the last 25 years roughly a fifth drier than the
mid-century baseline. Every one of the seven stations shows it. The decline
itself isn't controversial; what carries nuance is the cause, which the science
attributes largely to a strengthening high-pressure ridge and shifting storm
tracks under greenhouse and ozone forcing. For a water utility or a farm lender
that's textbook AASB S2 chronic physical risk: a permanent shift in the baseline,
not a run of bad years."
