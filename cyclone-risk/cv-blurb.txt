Physical Climate Risk: Tropical Cyclone Trends Affecting Western Australia

--- Three-sentence CV blurb (drop-in) ---
I analysed 40 years of IBTrACS best-track and NOAA ERSSTv5 sea-surface-temperature
data in Python (758 South Indian Ocean systems, 194 affecting Western Australia)
to test whether the intensity of WA-affecting tropical cyclones changed between
1985 and 2024, and whether it tracks ocean warming, framed as an AASB S2
physical-risk assessment. I found that although the seas off WA warmed about
0.5 degrees C over the period, WA-affecting cyclone intensity did not increase (a
weak, mostly non-significant decline) and showed no positive correlation with
local sea-surface temperature, indicating that physical-risk assessment must rely
on forward-looking climate projections rather than extrapolation of the recent
observed record. I delivered a fully reproducible analysis, with the statistical
routines (Mann-Kendall, Sen's slope, OLS regression) implemented from first
principles and validated against textbook values, plus six publication-quality
charts, published on GitHub.


--- Interview headline (say this in about 15 seconds) ---
"I asked whether tropical cyclones hitting WA have gotten stronger as the ocean
warmed. Over 1985 to 2024 the regional sea surface warmed about half a degree, but
the WA-affecting cyclones did not intensify. If anything they weakened slightly,
and there was no positive link to sea-surface temperature. The honest takeaway for
climate risk is that you cannot just extrapolate 'warmer equals stronger' from the
local record; you need forward-looking projections, which is exactly the judgement
AASB S2 physical-risk disclosure asks companies to make."
