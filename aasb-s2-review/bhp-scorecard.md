# BHP - AASB S2 Readiness Scorecard (FY2025)

Scored from the original documents: BHP Annual Report 2025 (OFR 9.8 Climate change, plus the Risk, Corporate Governance and Remuneration sections), Climate Transition Action Plan 2024 (CTAP 2024), GHG Emissions Calculation Methodology 2025, and the ESG Standards and Databook 2025. The full text was pulled out locally with pdftotext, and citations point to the reports' own section and page references. (AASB S2 is Australia's new mandatory rules that require big companies to disclose climate-related information.)

## Reporting status (important context)

BHP's Annual Report 2025 says its first **mandatory** AASB S2 report will be for **FY2026**. So this FY2025 disclosure is voluntary and follows TCFD (the Task Force on Climate-related Financial Disclosures, the older, voluntary framework). BHP maps its report to the 11 recommended TCFD disclosures, not yet to the AASB S2 format. The scores below measure how ready BHP is for the AASB S2 bar that starts to apply to it next year.

BHP sits at the mature end of voluntary disclosure. It has a credible, Board-approved transition plan and unusually complete Scope 3 reporting (Scope 3 is the emissions from the rest of the value chain, including customers using its products, not from BHP's own operations). But it has the gaps in hard numbers that AASB S2 will expose.

## Strengths (scored 4)

- **Transition plan (S4).** CTAP 2024 is a full, Board-approved transition plan. OFR 9.8 reports FY2025 progress honestly and revises the pathway to allow for technology delays.
- **Scope 3 disclosure (M3).** A full Scope 3 inventory (~378 MtCO2-e FY2025) broken down by category, with 84% openly stated as coming from iron ore and steelmaking coal processing. Most of the companies in this review are weak here; BHP is strong.
- **Scope 1 disclosure (M1)** and **named governance oversight (G1).** (Scope 1 is the emissions BHP makes directly from its own operations.)
- A genuine strength inside M12: the operational target is met through **structural abatement, not offsets** (the report says this explicitly), and it lines up with a modelled 1.5C scenario. (Structural abatement means actually cutting emissions, rather than paying for cuts elsewhere to offset them.)

## Key gaps (scored 2, where AASB S2 will expose BHP)

- **Putting numbers on financial effects (S5).** Spending on decarbonisation is quantified, but the wider financial effects on BHP's position, performance and cash flows are limited. This is the single most common gap across the whole project.
- **% of assets at risk from transition and physical risk (M4, M5)** and **% lined up with opportunities (M6).** These are discussed in words, but not put into the asset-percentage figures that the AASB S2 cross-industry metrics expect. (Cross-industry metrics are the standard, comparable figures every company must report. Transition risk is the risk from the shift to a low-carbon economy; physical risk is the risk from the physical effects of climate change, such as floods and heat.)
- **Internal carbon price (M8).** BHP has disclosed that it uses internal carbon price assumptions since the 2010s, but it does not clearly state the price per tonne. (An internal carbon price is a made-up cost per tonne of emissions that a company applies to its own decisions.)
- **Remuneration link (M9).** A decarbonisation milestone sits in the social value scorecard, but the explicit percentage of executive incentive pay tied to climate was not confirmed.
- **No firm Scope 3 target (inside M12).** Net zero Scope 3 by CY2050 is described as an uncertain "goal", not a target with a baseline and interim steps.

## Items flagged for your review / verification

These scores rest on sections checked by cross-reference rather than read line by line, or on figures I could not fully confirm. Worth a look before locking them in:

- G2 (board climate skills) and G5/G6 (remuneration link, management role): these sit in the Corporate Governance Statement (p.87-100) and the Remuneration Report (p.104-112), which I cross-referenced but did not read in full.
- S6: confirm whether BHP models a high-warming (>2.5C) physical scenario, which AASB S2's two-scenario rule requires alongside the 1.5C scenario.
- M2: confirm both location-based and market-based Scope 2 in the Databook. (Scope 2 is the emissions from the electricity BHP buys; location-based and market-based are two accepted ways of measuring it.)
- M8: confirm whether a specific internal carbon price per tonne is disclosed anywhere.

## Note on method

The scoring is deliberately cautious: where I was unsure between two scores, I took the lower one and said why in the matrix `gap_note`. Confidence is tagged for each row in `scoring-matrix.csv`. This measures how well BHP discloses, not how ready it actually is.
