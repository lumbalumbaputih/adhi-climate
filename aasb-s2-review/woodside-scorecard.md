# Woodside Energy - AASB S2 Readiness Scorecard (CY2025)

Scored from the original documents: Woodside Annual Report 2025 (the Sustainability Report, sections 3.6.2 Governance, 3.6.3 Risk management, 3.6.7-3.6.8 Climate, and the Remuneration Report) and the 2025 Climate and Sustainability Summary. The full text was pulled out locally with pdftotext, and citations point to the report's own section and page references. (AASB S2 is Australia's new mandatory rules that require big companies to disclose climate-related information.)

## Reporting status (important context)

Woodside closes its books in December (it is a calendar-year reporter) and is a Group 1 entity (one of the largest companies, which have to start reporting first). **So its CY2025 report is its FIRST MANDATORY AASB S2 disclosure.** The report says so plainly: "as a calendar year reporter, Woodside is required under the Corporations Act 2001 (Cth) and AASB S2 to include specific climate-related disclosures in this Sustainability Report." Unlike BHP (whose FY2025 report is still voluntary and follows TCFD, the older voluntary framework), Woodside is already reporting under the mandatory rules. You can see that in how its disclosure is structured and how complete it is.

## The key insight: strong disclosure is not the same as low climate risk

Woodside scores higher than BHP on disclosure readiness (3.4 vs 3.0), because as a first-time mandatory reporter its disclosure is already built to the AASB S2 structure. But how well a company discloses and how much climate risk it faces are two different things. The same report shows a strategy built on growing its LNG business (Scarborough, Louisiana LNG), and the transition risk in that strategy is exactly what AASB S2 exists to bring to light. (LNG is liquefied natural gas, a fossil fuel. Transition risk is the risk from the shift to a low-carbon economy.) An ESG analyst's job is to make that distinction: Woodside discloses well and is still heavily exposed. That contrast is the most useful talking point this company offers. (ESG stands for environmental, social and governance.)

## Strengths (scored 4)

- **Governance (G1, G3-G6).** Board plus Sustainability and Audit & Risk Committees; clear lines of management accountability; strategic risks reviewed at least twice a year.
- **Internal carbon price (M8).** An explicit US$80/t CO2-e (real 2024), applied in deciding where to put capital and which abatement to do, with methane effectively priced at about US$6,720/t. Most companies do not state a price at all. (An internal carbon price is a made-up cost per tonne of emissions that a company applies to its own decisions.)
- **Remuneration link (M9, G5).** Climate is an explicit 15% of the executive Company Scorecard (gross Scope 1+2 plus progress on new energy), and offsets are excluded from that measure. It is quantified and credible. (Scope 1 is the emissions Woodside makes directly; Scope 2 is the emissions from the electricity it buys. Offsets are payments for emissions cuts elsewhere to cancel out its own.)
- **Performance and assurance (M13).** The 2025 net target was met; gross Scope 1+2 (6,616 kt) was externally assured (checked by an outside party).
- **Risk integration (R6)** and **risk identification (R1).**

## Key gaps and credibility flags

- **Reliance on offsets (M12).** The net-equity Scope 1+2 target is met partly with carbon credits (1,283 kt retired to reach the 5,334 kt net figure against 6,616 kt gross). Woodside is transparent about this and puts abatement first, but the net target still leans on offsets.
- **No absolute Scope 3 target (M12).** There is only a Scope 3 *investment* target (US$5bn by 2030) and an *abatement* target (5 Mtpa), plus intensity framing (emissions per unit of output). For an LNG producer, where Scope 3 is the biggest part of the footprint, the lack of an absolute target to cut Scope 3 emissions is the headline gap. (Scope 3 is the emissions from the rest of the value chain, including customers burning the gas Woodside sells, not from Woodside's own operations.)
- **%-of-assets-at-risk metrics (M4, M5, M6).** Transition and physical risk are discussed in words, but not put into figures as an amount or percentage of assets. (Physical risk is the risk from the physical effects of climate change, such as floods and heat.)
- **Net zero 2050 is an "aspiration"** (the report's own word), not a committed plan.

## Items flagged for your review / verification

- S6: confirm whether Woodside models a high-warming (>2.5C) physical scenario alongside its 1.5C pathway, as AASB S2's two-scenario rule requires (look in s3.6.8 / Basis of Preparation).
- M2: confirm both location-based and market-based Scope 2 in the climate data table. (These are two accepted ways of measuring Scope 2 emissions.)
- M3: confirm the Scope 3 category breakdown in the climate data table.
- M10: confirm explicit SASB mapping (Ipieca/IOGP/GRI are confirmed). (These are all reporting frameworks the company can map its disclosures to.)

## Note on method

Cautious scoring; where I was unsure between two scores, I took the lower one with a reason in the matrix `gap_note`. Confidence is tagged for each row. This measures how well Woodside discloses, not how ready it actually is.
